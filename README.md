# Decision Communication

Formats for getting decisions made: options briefs, one-pagers, confirmation documents, talking points, and status updates, all built for readers who scan and decide rather than read and admire. From enterprise and government delivery, where the customer's decision *is* the deliverable and everything else is preparation.

## The Premise

Delivery teams produce analysis. Customers produce decisions. The gap between those two is where programs stall: the analysis was right, the deck was thorough, and the decision still didn't happen, or happened softly and got relitigated a month later.

Decision communication treats that gap as a craft with rules:

1. **Lead with what you need and when.** Bottom line up front, always. Busy readers decide in the first two lines whether the rest gets read.
2. **Criteria before options.** State how the choice should be judged before showing any choice. Criteria first makes the room evaluate; options first makes the room advocate.
3. **Show every trade out loud.** An option with no visible downside reads as either dishonest or not understood. Naming the cost yourself is what makes your recommendation trustworthy.
4. **Recommend, with reasoning showing.** You've seen the whole board. Say what you'd choose and why in two sentences. The customer can overrule a recommendation; they can't act on a shrug.
5. **A decision they understand is a decision that holds.** The test of every format here: can the decision-maker defend the choice a month later, without you in the room? If not, you got a nod, not a decision.

## The Formats

| Format | Use When |
|---|---|
| [Options Brief](formats/01-options-brief.md) | A significant choice with 2-4 viable paths: criteria, comparison matrix, detail cards, recommendation |
| [Decision One-Pager](formats/02-decision-one-pager.md) | One decision, one page, ninety seconds to read: the workhorse format |
| [Confirmation Brief](formats/03-confirmation-brief.md) | You need the customer to validate your understanding before a consequential step: "here's what we believe; is this right?" |
| [Talking Points](formats/04-talking-points.md) | The decision happens in a live conversation: points to land, objections pre-answered, the ask |
| [Status That Scans](formats/05-status-that-scans.md) | Recurring updates that maintain trust: health cards, no surprises, decisions surfaced early |
| [Slide Storyline & Critique](formats/06-slide-storyline-and-critique.md) | The decision needs a deck: sequence the story before building, de-clutter after |

## The Underlying Skill

Every format here is the same move at different scales: **do the customer's thinking-work for them without doing their choosing-work for them.** Structure the information so the decision is easy to make and hard to misunderstand, then ask for it explicitly, with a date. That's the whole craft. The formats are just that sentence, applied.
