# Worked Example: A Contested Retirement, Decided

A [Confirmation Brief](../formats/03-confirmation-brief.md) in action, driving a contested legacy-system retirement to documented agreement. Same **fictional** program as my other repositories: the municipal Fleet Maintenance System (FMS) replacing LegacyFleet. All content invented.

## The Situation

FMS is nearly ready to replace LegacyFleet, but the shop supervisors who mastered the old system over fifteen years don't believe it. Every retirement conversation so far has produced the same stalemate: "the new system doesn't do what we need," with no list of what "what we need" means. The program can't schedule a sunset date against a feeling.

## The Brief (as circulated before the session)

---

> ### LegacyFleet Retirement: Confirming What's Actually In Use
>
> We're excited to spend most of our two days on modernizing FMS to absorb the remaining LegacyFleet capabilities, and that's where the bulk of the agenda goes. But first we need to settle the unglamorous prerequisite: exactly what LegacyFleet does for you today.
>
> **Our best current understanding of the capabilities in daily use:**
>
> 1. Creating, modifying, and closing work orders
> 2. The vehicle history screen (reviewing a vehicle's full maintenance record)
> 3. Flagging vehicles (out-of-service and watch-list flags)
> 4. Routing work orders with attachments for approval
> 5. Importing the quarterly fleet allocation file
> 6. Adjusting allocations mid-quarter when circumstances change
> 7. The inbound/outbound vehicle transfer roster
>
> **Is this right?** We're asking only about capabilities in actual use in LegacyFleet today, not your entire process. If something's missing from this list, that's the most valuable thing you can tell us in this session.
>
> **If the list is right, here's how close we are:**
>
> | # | Capability | Status in FMS |
> |---|---|---|
> | 1 | Work orders | Complete by end of Sprint 34 at the latest |
> | 2 | Vehicle history | **Covered today.** Not called "history screen"; this is the Vehicle Record in FMS |
> | 3 | Flags | **Not complete.** Needs your input on how watch-list flags are actually used before we build |
> | 4 | Routing + attachments | **Covered today, fully** |
> | 5 | Allocation import | Handled upstream; the file now arrives to FMS already cleaned and validated |
> | 6 | Mid-quarter adjustments | Possible today by editing allocation records; we need clarity on what "adjusting" includes for you |
> | 7 | Transfer roster | **Covered today, fully** |
>
> **The question that decides this:** If we deliver all of the above, and we turn LegacyFleet off, can you still do your job, including with the workarounds you use today?
>
> - **If yes:** this becomes the LegacyFleet Retirement Plan. Next step: we draft the formal plan with dates for your approval.
> - **If no:** what other capabilities do you absolutely need? Those go on the record today and enter intake as first-class items.

---

## What Happened, and Why

Structurally, the brief did four things the previous conversations couldn't:

1. **It converted "the new system doesn't do what we need" into a seven-row table.** The supervisors corrected row 6 (mid-quarter adjustments turned out to include a reallocation approval step nobody had mapped) and added an eighth capability the program had missed entirely. Both went on the record as intake items. Neither had ever surfaced in the stalemate conversations, because there was no list to correct.
2. **The honest "not complete" rows bought the "covered today" rows their credibility.** Flag handling (row 3) admitted as unfinished is what made "routing is fully covered" believable. A table of seven greens would have been dismissed on sight.
3. **The keystone question changed what was being decided.** Not "do you like FMS" but "with this list delivered, can you work." The supervisors, authors of the now-corrected list, said yes with two conditions, both already on the record. That's not a softer no; it's an agreement with a punch list.
4. **Both exits were dignified.** The "if no" path fed intake rather than reopening the fight, so nobody had to defend LegacyFleet to avoid being steamrolled. People agree faster when agreeing isn't a corner.

The retirement plan drafted from that session carried the supervisors' own corrections in it. When a new stakeholder challenged the sunset two months later, the program answered with the signed brief, and the supervisors backed it, because it was theirs. **That's the standard for decision communication: the decision holds, defended by the people who made it, without you in the room.**
