# 01 – Options Brief

For significant choices with two to four viable paths: an architecture direction, a build-vs-configure call, a feature approach with real cost differences. The structure mirrors decision briefings your customer already knows how to consume: problem, criteria, options compared, recommendation, ask.

## Structure

### 1. Problem / Opportunity
What gap this decision closes, the impact of not deciding, and the scope boundary (what's explicitly out). Three to five sentences. If the problem statement runs longer, the decision isn't framed yet.

### 2. Evaluation Criteria
The factors the options will be judged against: cost, speed, complexity, risk, scalability, sustainment burden. Weighted if the audience will tolerate it, listed if not. **This section comes before any option appears**, which is the single most important structural rule in the format. Criteria first, and the audience judges. Options first, and the audience picks a side, then bends the criteria to fit.

### 3. Comparison Matrix
One table, options as columns, criteria as rows, color-coded for a fast read (green/yellow/red or equivalent). This is the anchor of the whole brief; many decision-makers will read only this and the recommendation. Build it so that's enough.

### 4. Option Detail Cards
One card per option, identical layout, so differences are differences of substance rather than formatting:

- Approach in three sentences
- Technical requirements and dependencies
- Timeline by phase
- Estimated effort and cost
- Top two pros, top two cons (exactly two of each; forced symmetry keeps the cards honest)
- Risk rating with the driving risk named

### 5. Recommendation
Which option, tied explicitly back to the criteria, in a short paragraph. If a hybrid is genuinely better, say so, but a hybrid recommended to avoid choosing is visible from across the room and costs you credibility.

### 6. Decision & Next Steps
The explicit ask, the date it's needed by, what happens on each answer, and the first three actions with owners once decided.

## Delivery Notes

- **Format follows the room.** A formal leadership setting wants slides; a record-of-decision context wants a document; a working session wants something you can edit live. Same structure regardless.
- **Pair the deck with a one-page summary** (see [Decision One-Pager](02-decision-one-pager.md)) for the decision authority who wasn't in the room. Decisions travel; briefs should travel with them.
- **Write the losing options fairly.** The person whose preferred option loses will reread your brief looking for a strawman. When they don't find one, the decision holds. That's the point.
