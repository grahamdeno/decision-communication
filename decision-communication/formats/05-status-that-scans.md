# 05 – Status That Scans

Recurring status is where delivery credibility is built or spent. The failure mode is universal: narrative paragraphs the sponsor has to *read* to find out whether anything needs them. Sponsors don't read status. They scan it, looking for exactly two things: is anything on fire, and is anything waiting on me.

Build the update to answer those two questions in ten seconds, and put everything else behind them.

## The Health-Card Layout

For a program with multiple workstreams, one card per workstream, identical layout, all visible at once:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ [WORKSTREAM NAME]           [STATUS COLOR]  │
│ Phase / % complete   ·   Trend ▲ ▼ ▬        │
│ One line: the thing to know this period      │
│ Needs from you: [decision / nothing]         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Five workstreams, five cards, one screen. The sponsor scans the colors, reads the one-liners on anything yellow or red, and finds their name only where a decision is actually waiting. That's the entire transaction, and it takes less time than reading one narrative paragraph.

## Rules

- **Status colors are earned, not negotiated.** Define the thresholds once (green: on plan; yellow: at risk, mitigation in motion; red: needs intervention) and hold them. A program where everything is green until suddenly red has a reporting problem before it has a delivery problem.
- **The trend arrow is often more informative than the color.** Green-and-declining warrants a different conversation than yellow-and-improving. Two characters, real signal.
- **"Needs from you" is the highest-value field on the card.** Most updates bury the ask in paragraph four. Give it a labeled home, and put "nothing" there proudly when it's true; that word is what teaches the sponsor your updates respect their time.
- **Depth goes behind the scan, not in it.** Card layer for everyone, then a per-workstream detail section for whoever clicks through. Layered, not blended.

## The No-Surprise Rhythm

The layout handles each update; the rhythm handles the relationship. Two practices:

1. **Fixed cadence, kept even when quiet.** An update that arrives every time, on time, including the boring weeks, is what makes the alarming one credible when it comes.
2. **Bad news travels at the speed of the mitigation, with options attached.** A risk raised mid-period with two options and a recommendation is a management conversation. The same risk revealed at the deadline is an incident. Same facts, different timing, opposite outcomes. The status format exists so that the early version is easy to send.

## The Test

Hand a period's update to someone outside the program. If they can tell you within thirty seconds which workstream is in trouble and what the sponsor owes, the format works. If they start reading from the top, it doesn't.
