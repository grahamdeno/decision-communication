# 04 – Talking Points

For decisions that happen in live conversation: a sensitive naming issue, a scope disagreement, a direction change the customer needs to hear in person before they'll read anything. The format is built to be glanced at mid-call, not read aloud.

## Structure

### 1. The opening (write it nearly verbatim)
The first thirty seconds set whether this is a collaboration or a defense. Write the actual opening sentences, especially for sensitive topics, and lead with whatever deference or reassurance is genuinely true: what you're *not* proposing to change, whose authority you're operating under, what stays exactly as it is.

### 2. The points to land (three, maximum)
Each point is a bolded claim followed by two or three supporting sentences you can compress or expand live. Three because a live conversation reliably transmits about three things; a fourth point doesn't add, it dilutes.

### 3. Objections, pre-answered
The section that separates talking points from a script. For each objection you'd bet on hearing:

- The objection, phrased the way *they'd* phrase it
- Your answer in two to four sentences, ending somewhere collaborative

Writing these in advance does two jobs: you respond composed instead of improvising under pressure, and drafting them is a self-test. An objection you can't answer cleanly on paper is a sign the position needs work before the meeting, not during it.

### 4. The visual on standby
One simple table or diagram, ready to share if the distinction isn't landing verbally. Abstract disagreements often dissolve when the structure is visible: a nesting model, a before/after, a two-column comparison. Prepare it; deploy it only if needed.

### 5. The ask and the exit
What you want from this conversation, stated in one sentence you can say naturally. And the fallback: if the answer is no or not-yet, what's the graceful next step that keeps the door open. Never enter a sensitive conversation with only one acceptable outcome.

## Delivery Notes

- **Glanceable formatting is the whole point.** Bold claims, short lines, white space. In the call you'll see the page for two seconds at a time; format for that reality.
- **Rehearse the opening and the ask out loud once.** Those two moments carry the meeting, and both get worse when improvised.
- **Log the outcome within the hour.** Live decisions evaporate. Two sentences to the decision log or a follow-up note ("confirming what we agreed") converts a good conversation into a durable decision.
